What is a CWA?

— and why should you care

The Basics

CWA stands for Cumulative Weighted Average. It is Ghana's primary academic performance metric — used by KNUST and some Ghanaian universities. Think of it as your academic GPA, but measured out of 100 instead of 4.0.

The "weighted" part is key. Not all courses count equally. A 4-credit course like MATH 151 pulls your CWA four times harder than a 1-credit course. Every mark matters — but some marks matter more.

CWA = Σ(Score × Credits) ÷ Σ(Credits attempted)

Example: You score 80 in MATH 151 (4cr) and 60 in ENGL 157 (2cr). Your CWA is not simply (80+60)÷2 = 70. It is (80×4 + 60×2) ÷ (4+2) = (320+120)÷6 = 73.3. The heavier course has more pull.

Grade Classes

At the end of your four years, your overall CWA determines your degree classification. These are the thresholds at KNUST:

≥ 70 First Class Honours — the top tier. Rare. Respected everywhere.
60–69 Second Class Upper — strong, competitive. Opens most doors.
50–59 Second Class Lower — a pass. Some roles require Upper or above.
< 50 Below Pass — re-sit territory. Not where you want to be.

The student with the highest CWA across their entire graduating class becomes the Valedictorian — the one who gives the graduation speech, carries the department's honour, and has that achievement follow them for life. It is not just a GPA. It is a title.

CWA vs GPA — What's the difference?

Both measure academic performance using course weights, but they come from different traditions and look different on paper:

CWA (Ghana / UK-style)

Scored out of 100

Used across Ghanaian universities

First Class = ≥70

Reflects UK honours tradition

Granular — 73.5 vs 74.1 matters

GPA (US-style)

Scored out of 4.0

Standard across US universities

Magna Cum Laude ≈ 3.7+

Reflects US letter-grade system

Compressed — 3.8 vs 3.9 is small

Schools that adopt the GPA system (MIT, Harvard, most US institutions) find it simpler to compare across departments. Schools that use CWA (Oxford-influenced, KNUST) argue the 100-point scale allows more precise differentiation — especially important when ranking students for competitive positions, scholarships, or valedictorian selection.

Neither is superior. They are different lenses on the same question: how well did you understand what you were taught?

How this Calculator Works

It applies the exact KNUST CWA formula. Enter your score for each course, and it computes the weighted average in real time. Courses you leave blank are excluded from the calculation — so partial results are still meaningful.

Your CWA = Σ(your score × credit hours) ÷ Σ(credit hours entered)

The Target CWA tab works in reverse — you set the CWA you want (say, 70 for First Class) and it tells you exactly what score you need in each remaining course to get there, given what you've already entered.

AI 150 is toggleable because it's a new university-wide course whose inclusion in official CWA calculations varies. Toggle it on if your department includes it; off if not.

Nothing is stored. No server. No database. Every calculation lives entirely in your browser. Close the tab and it's gone — unless you export it.

Why I Built This

It was somewhere between the third week of lectures and the first round of test results that I realised most of us had no idea where we actually stood.

We didn't know what they meant in aggregate. We didn't know how a 55 in a 4-credit course was quietly dragging the whole CWA down, or how a strong performance in MATH 151 could pull everything up.

I'm Asante Kwaku Okyere — a first-year Biomedical Engineering student at KNUST, Class of 2029. I built this as a side project because I wanted something simple, fast, and honest. Something that just shows you the number. No sign-up. No ads. No tracking.

I was also building the BME1 Student Portal at the same time — a full platform with timetables, attendance tracking, an AI assistant, and a live class constellation map. The CWA Calculator started as one feature inside the portal. It outgrew the portal. So I gave it its own home.

The design took its cues from financial terminals and editorial design — because your academic record is financial data, in a way. It compounds. Small improvements early have outsized impact later. Treat it that way.
